Physical Properties of Group 15 Elements

Group 15 elements: Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), Bismuth (Bi). All have the outer electron configuration ns¬≤np¬≥ ‚ÄĒ giving 5 valence electrons and 3 unpaired electrons (by Hund's rule).

Trends Down Group 15

  • Atomic radius ‚ÄĒ increases (more electron shells)
  • Ionisation energy ‚ÄĒ generally decreases, but note N has higher IE‚āĀ than O (half-filled 2p¬≥ = extra stability)
  • Electronegativity ‚ÄĒ decreases: N (3.0) ‚Üí P (2.1) ‚Üí As (2.0) ‚Üí Sb (1.9) ‚Üí Bi (1.9)
  • Metallic character ‚ÄĒ increases (N = typical non-metal; Bi = metal)

Why is nitrogen's IE‚āĀ higher than oxygen's? Nitrogen 2p¬≥ is half-filled (one electron per orbital ‚ÄĒ Hund's rule). This is an extra-stable configuration. Nitrogen ‚ÄĒ Occurrence and Inertness

Occurrence of Nitrogen

Nitrogen makes up 78.1% of the atmosphere (by volume) as diatomic N‚āā. It is also found in proteins, nucleic acids (DNA, RNA), and minerals (saltpetre NaNO‚āÉ, Chile saltpetre NaNO‚āÉ). The nitrogen cycle describes how nitrogen moves between the atmosphere, soil, and living organisms.

Structure of N‚āā ‚ÄĒ Why is Nitrogen so Inert?

Nitrogen exists as N‚Č°N ‚ÄĒ a triple bond: one ŌÉ bond and two ŌÄ bonds. The N‚Č°N bond is exceptionally strong: 945 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ (the strongest homonuclear diatomic bond). This makes N‚āā extremely unreactive at room temperature.

Reasons for N‚āā inertness:

  • Very high N‚Č°N bond energy (945 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ) ‚ÄĒ requires enormous activation energy to break
  • N‚āā is non-polar ‚ÄĒ no őī‚Āļ/őī‚ĀĽ centres for nucleophilic or electrophilic attack
  • The ŌÄ bonds prevent rotation and make the molecule rigid
  • N is small ‚ÄĒ steric hindrance prevents approach of reagents to the strongly bound nitrogen atoms

Reactions of Nitrogen

Despite its general inertness, N‚āā reacts under forcing conditions:

N‚āā + 3H‚āā ‚áĆ 2NH‚āÉ Haber Process (450¬įC, 200 atm, Fe catalyst) N‚āā + O‚āā ‚áĆ 2NO At very high T (lightning discharge; >2000¬įC) N‚āā + 3Mg ‚Üí Mg‚āÉN‚āā Heated Mg burns in N‚āā to form magnesium nitride N‚āā + 3Ca ‚Üí Ca‚āÉN‚āā Calcium also reacts when heated N‚āā + 2CaC‚āā ‚Üí CaCN‚āā + C Calcium cyanamide (fertiliser) ‚ÄĒ at 1000¬įC

Biological nitrogen fixation: certain bacteria (Rhizobium, Azotobacter) use the enzyme nitrogenase to convert N‚āā to NH‚āÉ at room temperature and pressure ‚ÄĒ a remarkable feat that chemistry struggles to replicate industrially.

Ammonia (NH‚āÉ)

Structure and Properties of Ammonia

Ammonia has the molecular formula NH‚āÉ. Nitrogen is sp¬≥ hybridised with one lone pair and three N‚ÄďH bonds. The molecular shape is trigonal pyramidal with a bond angle of 107¬į (lone pair repels bonding pairs ‚Üí angle less than 109.5¬į).

Key properties of ammonia:

  • Colourless gas with pungent smell; b.p. = ‚ąí33¬įC
  • Highly soluble in water (700 volumes at STP) ‚ÄĒ forms alkaline solution: NH‚āÉ + H‚āāO ‚áĆ NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ
  • Hydrogen bonding ‚ÄĒ N‚ÄďH¬∑¬∑¬∑N bonds between molecules ‚Üí higher b.p. than PH‚āÉ (‚ąí88¬įC)
  • Acts as a Lewis base (lone pair donor) and a Br√łnsted-Lowry base (proton acceptor)
  • Forms complex ions with transition metals: Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 4NH‚āÉ ‚Üí [Cu(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄ]¬≤‚Āļ (deep blue)

Industrial Manufacture ‚ÄĒ The Haber Process

The Haber Process manufactures ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen:

N‚āā(g) + 3H‚āā(g) ‚áĆ 2NH‚āÉ(g) őĒH = ‚ąí92 kJ mol‚ĀĽ¬Ļ (exothermic)

Conditions:

  • Temperature: ~450¬įC ‚ÄĒ compromise between yield (lower T favours product) and rate (higher T gives faster reaction)
  • Pressure: ~200 atm ‚ÄĒ high pressure favours product (4 moles gas ‚Üí 2 moles gas); limited by engineering/economic constraints
  • Catalyst: Finely divided iron (Fe) with promoters K‚āāO and Al‚āāO‚āÉ
  • Recycling: Unreacted N‚āā and H‚āā are recycled ‚ÄĒ yield per pass is only ~15%; overall conversion ~97%

1

3

N‚āā remaining = 1‚ąí0.261 = 0.739 mol. Total = 4‚ąí2(0.261) = 3.478 mol. Mole fraction of N‚āā = 0.739/3.478 = 0.212 (21.2%).

Nitric Acid (HNO‚āÉ)

Industrial Manufacture ‚ÄĒ The Ostwald Process

Nitric acid is manufactured from ammonia by the Ostwald Process in three steps:

Step 1: 4NH‚āÉ + 5O‚āā ‚Üí 4NO + 6H‚āāO (Pt/Rh catalyst, ~850¬įC, 5‚Äď10 atm) Step 2: 2NO + O‚āā ‚Üí 2NO‚āā (cooling to ~50¬įC ‚ÄĒ NO oxidised in air) Step 3: 3NO‚āā + H‚āāO ‚Üí 2HNO‚āÉ + NO (absorbed in water ‚ÄĒ NO recycled to step 2)

Concentrated HNO‚āÉ is ~68% HNO‚āÉ. It can be made more concentrated by distillation with H‚āāSO‚āĄ.

Nitric acid is a strong acid (fully ionises: HNO‚āÉ ‚Üí H‚Āļ + NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ) and a strong oxidising agent. The products of reaction with metals depend on the concentration and the metal.

Why can P form PCl‚āÖ but N cannot form NCl‚āÖ? Phosphorus has accessible empty 3d orbitals ‚ÄĒ it can expand its octet to accommodate 5 bonds (sp¬≥d hybridisation). Nitrogen is in Period 2 ‚ÄĒ no available d orbitals ‚ÄĒ maximum coordination number = 4. Therefore the maximum number of bonds N can form is 4 (e.g. NH‚āĄ‚Āļ), while P can form 5 (PCl‚āÖ) or 6.

    Step 1: 4NH‚āÉ + 5O‚āā ‚Üí 4NO + 6H‚āāO ‚ÄĒ Pt/Rh gauze catalyst, ~850¬įC, 5‚Äď10 atm. Step 2: 2NO + O‚āā ‚Üí 2NO‚āā ‚ÄĒ cooling the gas; NO is oxidised in air.   • Write equations to show: (a) the reaction of concentrated HNO‚āÉ with copper (b) the reaction of dilute HNO‚āÉ with copper. Explain the difference in the nitrogen oxide produced.

Dilute HNO‚āÉ ‚Üí N reduced from +5 to +2 (NO). Both reactions: HNO‚āÉ acts as oxidising acid; H‚Āļ + NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ oxidises the metal.
