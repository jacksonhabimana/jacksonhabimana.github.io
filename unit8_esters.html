Esters, Acid Anhydrides, Amides and Nitriles

Structure · Nomenclature · Preparation · Properties · Saponification · Detergents

8.1

Esters

EstersEsters have the functional group –COO– (ester linkage). General formula: RCOOR'. IUPAC name: alkyl alkanoate (R' part first, then RCOO⁻ part as –oate).

Nomenclature of Esters

Name = (alkyl from alcohol) + (alkanoate from acid).
CH₃COOCH₃ = methyl ethanoate (methanol + ethanoic acid)
CH₃COOC₂H₅ = ethyl ethanoate (ethanol + ethanoic acid)
C₂H₅COOCH₃ = methyl propanoate (methanol + propanoic acid)
HCOOC₂H₅ = ethyl methanoate (ethanol + methanoic acid)

Physical Properties of Esters

Boiling points: Lower than corresponding acids (no H-bonding between ester molecules). Similar to aldehydes/ketones of same Mr.

Solubility: Slightly soluble in water; dissolve well in organic solvents.

Odour: Pleasant fruity or floral smells. Used extensively as food flavourings and perfumes.

Preparation of Esters

1. Fischer Esterification (acid + alcohol):

RCOOH + R'OH ⇌(conc H₂SO₄, reflux) RCOOR' + H₂O

2. Acyl chloride + alcohol (faster, no catalyst):

RCOCl + R'OH → RCOOR' + HCl

3. Acid anhydride + alcohol:

(RCO)₂O + R'OH → RCOOR' + RCOOH

Reactions of Esters

1. Hydrolysis (reversible with H₂O, acid catalyst):

RCOOR' + H₂O ⇌(H⁺, reflux) RCOOH + R'OH

2. Saponification (alkaline hydrolysis — irreversible):

RCOOR' + NaOH →(reflux) RCOONa + R'OH (soap formation from fats)

3. Transesterification:

RCOOR' + R''OH ⇌(acid/base) RCOOR'' + R'OH

4. Reduction with LiAlH₄:

RCOOR' + 4[H] →(LiAlH₄) RCH₂OH + R'OH (two alcohols)
8.2

Uses of Esters

Applications

Flavourings and perfumes: Volatile esters are widely used as artificial flavourings (fruit flavours) and in perfumes.

Solvents: Ethyl ethanoate — paint, nail polish remover; butyl ethanoate — lacquers.

Polymers: Polyester (PET) from ethane-1,2-diol + benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid (terephthalic acid) → used in drinks bottles, clothing fibres (Terylene/Dacron).

Fats and oils (triesters of glycerol): Naturally occurring esters. Hydrolysis = soap-making.

Aspirin: Acetylsalicylic acid is an ester used as analgesic/anti-inflammatory.

Biodiesel: Transesterification of vegetable oil triglycerides with methanol → methyl esters (FAME).

8.3

Saponification and Soaps

Soap Manufacture

Fats (triglycerides = triesters of glycerol + 3 fatty acids) are hydrolysed with NaOH to give soap (sodium carboxylate) + glycerol.

Triglyceride + 3NaOH → 3 RCOONa (soap) + HOCH₂CH(OH)CH₂OH (glycerol)

Structure of soap: Long non-polar hydrocarbon tail (hydrophobic) + polar carboxylate head (hydrophilic, ionic). This amphiphilic structure allows soap to form micelles around grease → grease is emulsified and washed away.

Hard water problem: Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions in hard water react with soap → insoluble precipitate (scum). Detergents use sulphonate or sulphate head groups instead → soluble Ca²⁺ salts → no scum.

8.4

Acid Anhydrides

Acid AnhydridesTwo carboxyl groups joined by elimination of water. Functional group: –CO–O–CO–. Name: replace "ic acid" with "ic anhydride" or "(acid name) anhydride".
Example: (CH₃CO)₂O = ethanoic anhydride (acetic anhydride)

Reactions of Acid Anhydrides

Similar to acyl chlorides but less vigorous (safer to handle).

(RCO)₂O + H₂O → 2 RCOOH (hydrolysis) (RCO)₂O + R'OH → RCOOR' + RCOOH (esterification) (RCO)₂O + NH₃ → RCONH₂ + RCOOH (amide formation) (RCO)₂O + R'NH₂ → RCONHR' + RCOOH (N-substituted amide)

Industrial use of ethanoic anhydride: Used instead of ethanoyl chloride for acetylation reactions (cheaper; no HCl produced). Key in synthesis of aspirin and cellulose acetate.

8.5

Amides

AmidesFunctional group: –CONH₂. Derived from carboxylic acid by replacing –OH with –NH₂. IUPAC: replace –oic acid with –amide.
CH₃CONH₂ = ethanamide; CH₃CH₂CONH₂ = propanamide

Properties and Reactions of Amides

Physical: High melting points (H-bonding via N–H). Low members (methanamide) are liquids; ethanamide bp = 221°C!

Reactions:

RCONH₂ + H₂O →(H⁺/OH⁻, reflux) RCOOH + NH₃ (hydrolysis) RCONH₂ + 4[H] →(LiAlH₄) RCH₂NH₂ + H₂O (reduction → primary amine) RCONH₂ + Br₂ + 4NaOH → RNH₂ + Na₂CO₃ + 2NaBr + 2H₂O (Hofmann degradation, chain –1C)

Nylon (polyamide): Formed by condensation polymerisation between diacid chlorides and diamines. Nylon-6,6 from hexanedioyl dichloride + 1,6-diaminohexane.

8.6

Nitriles

NitrilesContain the –C≡N (cyano) group. IUPAC: count C of CN, suffix –nitrile.
CH₃CN = ethanenitrile; CH₃CH₂CN = propanenitrile

Reactions of Nitriles

RCN + 2H₂O →(H⁺, reflux) RCOOH + NH₃ (hydrolysis to carboxylic acid) RCN + 4[H] →(LiAlH₄) RCH₂NH₂ (reduction to primary amine) RCN + H₂O →(partial, H⁺) RCONH₂ (partial hydrolysis to amide)

Preparation: RX + KCN →(ethanol) RCN + KX (nucleophilic substitution; chain extended by 1C)

Worked Examples

Example 1— Naming Esters

Q: Name: (a) CH₃COOCH₂CH₃  (b) HCOOCH(CH₃)₂  (c) CH₃CH₂COOCH₃

a
CH₃COO = ethanoate part (from ethanoic acid); OCH₂CH₃ = ethyl part → ethyl ethanoate
b
HCOO = methanoate; OCH(CH₃)₂ = propan-2-yl (isopropyl) → propan-2-yl methanoate
c
CH₃CH₂COO = propanoate; OCH₃ = methyl → methyl propanoate
Answers(a) ethyl ethanoate (b) propan-2-yl methanoate (c) methyl propanoate
Example 2— Saponification Equation

Q: A fat (tripalmitin) is saponified with NaOH. Write the equation. What are the products?

1
Tripalmitin = glyceryl tripalmitate = triester of glycerol + palmitic acid (C₁₅H₃₁COOH) × 3
2
(C₁₅H₃₁COO)₃C₃H₅ + 3NaOH → 3C₁₅H₃₁COONa + C₃H₅(OH)₃
Products:Sodium palmitate (soap) + glycerol
Example 3— Ester Hydrolysis

Q: Identify the acid and alcohol formed when methyl propanoate undergoes acid hydrolysis.

1
CH₃CH₂COOCH₃ + H₂O →(H⁺) CH₃CH₂COOH + CH₃OH
Products:Propanoic acid + methanol
Example 4— Amide Hydrolysis

Q: What products form when propanamide is hydrolysed with dilute HCl?

1
CH₃CH₂CONH₂ + H₂O →(H⁺, reflux) CH₃CH₂COOH + NH₃
2
NH₃ + HCl → NH₄Cl (in acidic conditions)
Products:Propanoic acid + ammonium chloride
Example 5— How Soap Works

Q: Explain how soap removes grease from hands, referring to the structure of a soap molecule.

1
Soap = RCOONa. Dissociates: RCOO⁻ + Na⁺. Has hydrophilic head (COO⁻) and hydrophobic tail (R = long hydrocarbon chain).
2
Soap molecules form micelles around grease droplets: hydrophobic tails point inward (into grease); hydrophilic heads face outward (into water).
3
Micelles are water-soluble → grease carried away by water.
Key terms:Amphiphilic structure, micelle formation, hydrophilic/hydrophobic

Exercises with Answers

Q1

Write equations for the preparation of ethyl propanoate by: (a) direct esterification; (b) using propanoyl chloride; (c) using propanoic anhydride.

(a) CH₃CH₂COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌(conc H₂SO₄, reflux) CH₃CH₂COOC₂H₅ + H₂O
(b) CH₃CH₂COCl + C₂H₅OH → CH₃CH₂COOC₂H₅ + HCl
(c) (CH₃CH₂CO)₂O + C₂H₅OH → CH₃CH₂COOC₂H₅ + CH₃CH₂COOH
Q2

Compare saponification (alkaline hydrolysis) with acid hydrolysis of esters, explaining why saponification is irreversible.

Acid hydrolysis: RCOOR' + H₂O ⇌(H⁺) RCOOH + R'OH — reversible equilibrium; acid is a catalyst.
Saponification: RCOOR' + NaOH → RCOONa + R'OH — irreversible; NaOH is consumed (not a catalyst). The carboxylate ion (RCOO⁻) formed is stabilised and does not re-esterify under alkaline conditions; the reaction is driven to completion.
Q3

Why do detergents work in hard water but soaps form scum?

Hard water contains Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions. Soaps (carboxylate salts RCOO⁻Na⁺) react with these ions: 2RCOO⁻ + Ca²⁺ → (RCOO)₂Ca↓ (insoluble scum — calcium carboxylate precipitate). Detergents use sulphonate (RSO₃⁻Na⁺) or sulphate (ROSO₃⁻Na⁺) head groups whose calcium and magnesium salts are soluble → no precipitate → effective in hard water.
Q4

What is the difference between a polyester and a polyamide? Give one example of each and state its monomer(s).

Polyester: Condensation polymer with ester (–COO–) linkages. Example: PET (Terylene) from ethane-1,2-diol + benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid (terephthalic acid). Used in drink bottles, polyester clothing.
Polyamide: Condensation polymer with amide (–CONH–) linkages. Example: Nylon-6,6 from hexanedioyl dichloride + 1,6-diaminohexane. Also Nylon-6 from caprolactam (ring-opening). Used in ropes, stockings, toothbrush bristles.
Q5

Starting from bromoethane, show how to synthesise ethanamide in 2 steps. Name all intermediates.

Step 1: CH₃CH₂Br + KCN →(ethanol) CH₃CH₂CN (propanenitrile) + KBr
Wait — bromoethane → ethanenitrile (not propanenitrile).
Actually: CH₃CH₂Br has 2C; KCN adds 1C → CH₃CH₂CN = propanenitrile (3C).
For ethanamide (2C amide), start differently: CH₃Br + KCN → CH₃CN (ethanenitrile); then partial hydrolysis: CH₃CN + H₂O →(H⁺) CH₃CONH₂ (ethanamide).
OR: from ethanoyl chloride: CH₃COCl + NH₃ → CH₃CONH₂ + HCl.
