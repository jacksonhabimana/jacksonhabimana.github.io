Acid-Base Theories

Arrhenius Theory (1884)

The earliest theory, applicable only to aqueous solutions:

Arrhenius Acid A substance that dissociates in water to produce H‚Āļ ions (protons). e.g. HCl ‚Üí H‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ; H‚āāSO‚āĄ ‚Üí 2H‚Āļ + SO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ

Limitations: Cannot explain why NH‚āÉ is a base (no OH‚ĀĽ in its formula), or acid-base reactions in non-aqueous solvents.

Br√łnsted-Lowry Theory (1923)

A broader theory based on proton (H‚Āļ) transfer:

Br√łnsted-Lowry Acid A proton (H‚Āļ) donor ‚ÄĒ loses a proton to another species.
Br√łnsted-Lowry Base A proton (H‚Āļ) acceptor ‚ÄĒ gains a proton from an acid.

In every Br√łnsted-Lowry acid-base reaction, a proton is transferred. There is always a conjugate acid-base pair:

HCl + H‚āāO ‚Üí H‚āÉO‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ acid‚āĀ + base‚āā ‚Üí acid‚āā + base‚āĀ (HCl/Cl‚ĀĽ is conjugate pair 1; H‚āÉO‚Āļ/H‚āāO is conjugate pair 2) NH‚āÉ + H‚āāO ‚áĆ NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ base‚āĀ + acid‚āā ‚Üí acid‚āĀ + base‚āā (NH‚āĄ‚Āļ/NH‚āÉ is conjugate pair 1; H‚āāO/OH‚ĀĽ is conjugate pair 2)
ūüí°
Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by ONE proton (H‚Āļ) HCl and Cl‚ĀĽ are a conjugate pair (differ by H‚Āļ). NH‚āĄ‚Āļ and NH‚āÉ are a conjugate pair. H‚āāO can act as either acid (donates H‚Āļ ‚Üí OH‚ĀĽ) or base (accepts H‚Āļ ‚Üí H‚āÉO‚Āļ) ‚ÄĒ water is amphiprotic.

Lewis Theory (1923)

The most general theory ‚ÄĒ based on electron pair donation:

Lewis Acid An electron pair acceptor ‚ÄĒ accepts a lone pair of electrons to form a coordinate bond.
Lewis Base An electron pair donor ‚ÄĒ donates a lone pair to a Lewis acid.
BF‚āÉ + NH‚āÉ ‚Üí F‚āÉB‚ÜźNH‚āÉ (BF‚āÉ = Lewis acid, has empty p orbital; NH‚āÉ = Lewis base) AlCl‚āÉ + Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí AlCl‚āĄ‚ĀĽ (AlCl‚āÉ = Lewis acid; Cl‚ĀĽ = Lewis base) Fe¬≥‚Āļ + 6H‚āāO ‚Üí [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ (Fe¬≥‚Āļ = Lewis acid; H‚āāO = Lewis base) Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 4NH‚āÉ ‚Üí [Cu(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄ]¬≤‚Āļ (Cu¬≤‚Āļ = Lewis acid; NH‚āÉ = Lewis base)

Lewis theory explains all Br√łnsted-Lowry reactions (H‚Āļ is a Lewis acid ‚ÄĒ accepts lone pair from a base) and extends to reactions without H‚Āļ transfer, including complex ion formation and many organic reactions.

Strong and Weak Acids and Bases

A strong acid is one that is completely (fully) dissociated in aqueous solution ‚ÄĒ all molecules ionise:

Examples of weak acids: CH‚āÉCOOH (ethanoic/acetic), HCN, H‚āāCO‚āÉ, H‚āāSO‚āÉ, H‚āÉPO‚āĄ, HF, organic carboxylic acids. Most are molecular organic acids.

Strong bases: completely ionised in water ‚ÄĒ Group 1 hydroxides (NaOH, KOH, LiOH) and Ca(OH)‚āā (sparingly soluble but fully ionised when dissolved).

Buffer Solutions

Definition ‚ÄĒ Buffer Solution A solution that resists significant changes in pH when small amounts of acid or alkali are added, or when the solution is diluted. It typically consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid) in comparable amounts.

Acidic Buffer ‚ÄĒ Weak Acid + Conjugate Base Salt

Example: CH‚āÉCOOH / CH‚āÉCOONa (ethanoic acid / sodium ethanoate). pH ‚Čą 4‚Äď6.

How it works:

  • When H‚Āļ added: CH‚āÉCOO‚ĀĽ(aq) + H‚Āļ(aq) ‚Üí CH‚āÉCOOH(aq). The conjugate base "mops up" added H‚Āļ ‚Üí pH barely changes.
  • When OH‚ĀĽ added: CH‚āÉCOOH(aq) + OH‚ĀĽ(aq) ‚Üí CH‚āÉCOO‚ĀĽ(aq) + H‚āāO. The weak acid "neutralises" added OH‚ĀĽ ‚Üí pH barely changes.

Buffer pH (Henderson-Hasselbalch equation):

pH = pKa + log([A‚ĀĽ] / [HA]) = pKa + log([salt] / [acid]) At equal concentrations of acid and salt: pH = pKa (simplest case)
ūüí°
A buffer is most effective when [HA] ‚Čą [A‚ĀĽ] Equal amounts of weak acid and its conjugate base ‚Üí pH = pKa. Buffer capacity is greatest at this point. Buffer is generally effective within ¬Ī1 pH unit of pKa.

Basic Buffer ‚ÄĒ Weak Base + Conjugate Acid Salt

Example: NH‚āÉ / NH‚āĄCl (ammonia / ammonium chloride). pH ‚Čą 8‚Äď10.

When H‚Āļ added: NH‚āÉ + H‚Āļ ‚Üí NH‚āĄ‚Āļ (NH‚āÉ mops up H‚Āļ) When OH‚ĀĽ added: NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ ‚Üí NH‚āÉ + H‚āāO (NH‚āĄ‚Āļ mops up OH‚ĀĽ) pH = 14 ‚ąí pKb ‚ąí log([NH‚āĄ‚Āļ]/[NH‚āÉ]) or use pKa of NH‚āĄ‚Āļ (9.25) in Henderson-Hasselbalch

  • Protein buffers: amino acid side chains (histidine imidazole, pKa ‚Čą 6.0) buffer inside cells

Definition ‚ÄĒ Buffer Solution A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. It maintains a nearly constant pH. Buffers contain a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid) in comparable concentrations.
